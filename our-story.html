The Story of Vayomeie

Born from a deep respect for ancient craft, a refusal to rush, and a belief that clothing should outlast seasons.

Scroll
Our Beginning

We started with a single question: Why does fast fashion exist when slow clothing feels so much better?

Vayomeie was born in Andhra Pradesh, a state that has woven and dyed cloth for centuries. We didn't need to invent a new process — we needed to honour the one that already existed.

Our name is a combination of the Sanskrit words for Earth and Spirit — Vayu and Meie. We believe every garment should carry both: grounded in the earth, alive with intention.

What We Stand For

Principles we will not
compromise on

01

Zero Overproduction

We make only what is ordered or what we can confidently sell. No waste, no surplus, no burning of unsold stock. Every piece we create will be worn.

02

Eco-Friendly Colours

Our dye stack comes entirely from plants and minerals — indigo, madder, pomegranate peel, myrobalan, iron, jaggery, and alum. Not a single synthetic dye in sight.

03

Breathable Natural Fabrics

Indian cotton, chosen for its breathability and its ability to carry natural dyes without chemical priming. The kind of fabric that improves with age.

04

Fair Artisan Wages

Our artisans are not contractors. They are partners. Paid above industry rate, with consistent work, and recognition for their craft. Their names matter to us.

05

Size-Inclusive Always

XS to 3XL on every design, without exception. The cost of inclusion is small. The message it sends is everything.

06

Designed to Last

Not a season. Not a year. Five years minimum — and better by then. We design for the wearer who counts their pieces, not their purchases.

The Making

Nine steps.
30 to 80 days.

Step 01

Sourcing the Cotton

We begin with Indian cotton — chosen by hand for its thread count, its breathability, and its capacity to hold natural colour without synthetic primers. The cloth arrives undyed, unmarked, full of potential.

1–3 days
Step 02

The Myrobalan Soak

Before any colour touches the cloth, it soaks in myrobalan — an ancient Indian mordant made from the dried fruit of the Terminalia chebula tree. This tannin-rich soak prepares the fibres to receive dye permanently.

2–4 days
Step 03

Block Printing by Hand

Our motifs are carved into teak blocks — some over 40 years old. Each impression is applied by hand, with care, pressure, and rhythm. No two prints are identical. That is the point.

3–7 days
Step 04

Sun Drying on Grass

After printing, the cloth is laid flat on grass under direct sun. The combination of sunlight and earth moisture helps the dye cure slowly and deeply. We don't use drying machines.

1–5 days (weather dependent)
Step 05

Boiling in Copper Vessels

The dyed fabric is boiled in large copper vessels with alum to fix and brighten the colour. Copper reacts with certain dyes to deepen their hue — a chemical relationship that aluminium and steel cannot replicate.

1–2 days
Step 06 – 09

Washing, Finishing, Cutting & Stitching

Four final steps — a thorough wash to remove excess dye, a hand-finishing pass to check every metre, precision cutting to our size templates, and careful stitching with double-seam reinforcement. The garment is then pressed, quality-checked, and ready to wear.

10–30 days
The Hands

Our artisans carry
centuries of knowledge

In the villages of Andhra Pradesh, the craft of natural dyeing and block printing has been passed down through families for generations. It is not taught in schools. It is inherited.

Our workshop is home to a core team of twelve artisans — printers, dyers, and finishers — each of whom has practised their craft for at least fifteen years. We don't train them; we learn from them.

When you wear a Vayomeie garment, you are wearing the knowledge of someone who spent a decade mastering the pressure behind a single block print.
